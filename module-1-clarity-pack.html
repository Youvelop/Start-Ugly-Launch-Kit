Part 1 of 3

The Idea Filter

You probably have more than one idea. That's the problem. Having five ideas and doing nothing is not a creativity problem. It's a decision problem. This filter forces you to pick one and kill the rest for now.

Take your top 3 to 5 ideas and run each one through these three questions.

Question 1

How much do I actually care? (1–10)

Not "is this a good idea." Not "could this make money." Do you actually care about it? Would you work on it at 6am or 10pm when you're exhausted? Would you still care if it took a year to get traction? Be honest. A 6 is a 6. Don't round up.

Question 2

Can I start with almost no money or resources? (Y/N)

Right now. With what you have. No big budget, no team, no special equipment. Can a version of this exist within the next 7 days? If you need to raise money, hire someone, or buy expensive tools before you can even start — that's a No.

Question 3

Will I still care about this in 6 months? (Y/N)

This is the filter that kills shiny object ideas. Some ideas feel urgent right now and will feel completely irrelevant by summer. Others have been sitting in the back of your mind for years and refuse to go away. Be honest about which category yours falls into.

My Idea How much do I care? (1–10) Can I start now? Still matters in 6 months?
If two ideas tie — ask yourself this and go with the first honest answer:
"If I could only work on one thing for the next 90 days and everything else was completely off the table, which one would I actually choose?"

The One Sentence
Offer Formula

Most people describe their offer the way they'd explain it at a family dinner. Vague, full of jargon, no clear outcome. Nobody buys that. This formula fixes it in one sentence.

The Formula
"I help [specific person] do [specific thing]
so they can [specific result]."
Blank 1

Who is the specific person?

Not "everyone." Not "people who want to improve their lives." A real person with a real problem. The more specific you are here the better your offer converts. Specificity feels like it shrinks your audience. It actually increases sales because people see themselves immediately.

Too vague

entrepreneurs

creatives

people who are stuck

Specific enough

first-time Etsy sellers

freelance designers with no consistent clients

new coaches who have never sold anything online

Blank 2

What is the specific thing you help them do?

One action. One outcome. Not a list of things.

Too vague

grow their business and mindset and brand

Specific enough

launch their first digital product

write and publish their first piece of content

set up a simple system to get their first 10 clients

Blank 3

What is the specific result they get?

What their life or business looks like after. Not during. After.

Too vague

feel more confident

grow as a person

Specific enough

make their first $500 online

have a product live and selling within a week

stop depending on one client for all their income

Your Offer — Live Preview
"I help ___ do ___ so they can ___."
Read it out loud. If a 12-year-old understands it, it's good. If it sounds like a LinkedIn bio, rewrite it.

Clean Up Your Idea
"I have a business idea but I'm struggling to describe it clearly. Here's my rough idea: [paste your messy description]. Help me turn this into one clear sentence that explains what I offer, who it's for, and what result they get. Keep it simple so everyone understands. No abstract language. No jargon. No BS. Write 3 versions and tell me which one you think is strongest and why."
